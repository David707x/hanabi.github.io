Level 16 - Dupe Tech

Conventions​


Duplicitous Value Clue​

  • It’s possible to pick up one or more valuable cards by tricking Bob into unknowingly playing a Duped card.
  • We agree that as long as such a clue meets Minimum Clue Value Principle (not counting the duplicated card itself), no further Prompts or Blind-Plays should be called for.
  • Instead, the team can immediately understand that this was simply a Duplicitous Value Clue, since it resulted in one or more extra valuable cards being Touched for safekeeping.
  • For example, in a 3-player game:
    • Blue 2 and Red 2 are played on the stacks.
    • Alice holds a known Red 3.
    • Alice clues 3 to Bob as a Play Clue, touching 2 cards.
    • Bob plays his focused 3, assuming it's Blue, but gasps in surprise when it's actually Red 3.
    • Although this was unexpected, the team sees that this was a Duplicitous Value Clue, since there is sufficient value in Good Touching Bob's other 3.
    • Bob marks his other 3 as any 3; it is not promised to be playable, so no one assumes they should Blind-Play or Prompt from the clue.
  • For Duplicitous Value Clues that do not meet MCVP, see the Dupe Tempo Clue Principle below.

Duplicitous Blind-Play​

  • As with the Duplicitous Value Clue, it's also possible to give a valuable clue that results in a Duped card being blind-played.
  • Once again, as long as the clue meets Minimum Clue Value Principle, no further Prompts or Blind-Plays should be called for.
    • In the past such a blind-play would cause Bob to continue to dig as a true Finesse, but this is no longer true; we agree that the creative Good Touching of the extra card(s) already provide sufficient justification for the clue.
  • For example, in a 4-player game:
    • Blue 2 and Red 2 are played on the stacks.
    • Alice holds a known Red 3.
    • Alice clues Blue to Donald, touching a Blue 4.
    • Bob Blind-Plays his First Finesse; it's Red 3.
    • Although this was unexpected, the team sees that this was a Duplicitous Blind-Play, since there is sufficient value in Good Touching Donald's Blue 4.
    • Therefore, the team understands that no further Blind-Plays are called for.
  • Note that Duplicitous Blind-Plays can also be performed with certain Ejections, Discharges, etc.

Dupe Tempo Clue Principle​

  • What if a Duplicitous clue doesn’t newly touch any additional valuable cards, but provides useful Fill-In information?
  • If a filled-in card is revealed as playable, this should retroactively be treated as a Tempo Clue.
    • This may or may not therefore cause a single Tempo Clue Chop Move, following the same condition guidelines as normal Tempo Clues.
  • For example, in a 3-player game:
    • All 1s are played on the stacks.
    • Cathy holds a known Red 2.
    • Bob holds a previously-touched Green 2, but its rank is not yet known.
    • Alice clues 2 to Bob, newly touching one card while filling in his Green 2.
    • Bob plays the newly touched 2, and is shocked that it's a Duped Red 2.
    • Since no new valuable cards were touched, Bob knows to treat this as a Dupe Tempo Clue.
    • Since his Green 2 was Promptable, and Alice was not in a valid Stalling situation, Bob treats this as a single Tempo Clue Chop Move.

Special Moves​


The Assisted Trash Chop Move​

  • Trash Chop Moves can be incredibly useful; but what if there’s no way for Alice to simply and cleanly clue known trash?
  • One clever solution is to perform an Assisted Trash Chop Move: this is when a Trash Bluff or (single-touch) Unknown Trash Discharge gets a known Dupe to blind-play.
  • The ultimate goal of such a clue will be clear to the team: communicate a Trash Chop Move on all cards to the right of the clued card(s).
  • For an example of an Assisted Trash Bluff, in a 3-player game:
    • Blue 1 is played on the stacks.
    • Alice holds a known Yellow 1.
    • Cathy's hand is r5, g3, b1, y4, g4.
    • Alice clues 1 to Cathy, as a Trash Bluff.
    • Bob and Cathy gasp as Bob blind-plays Yellow 1.
    • Alice's intention becomes clear to everyone: she wanted to provide Cathy with safe Known Trash, and expects Cathy to Trash Chop Move her two cards behind the b1.
  • For an example of an Assisted Trash Discharge, in a 3-player game:
    • The situation is the same as above.
    • Alice clues blue to Cathy, as an Unknown Trash Discharge.
    • Bob and Cathy gasp as Bob plays Yellow 1 from Third Finesse Position.
    • Cathy evaluates. If Alice's clue had touched a secondary blue card, this would simply be a Duplicitous Blind-Play.
    • But since Alice's clue only resulted in Known Trash being touched, Alice must have intended a Trash Chop Move on Cathy.

The Time Travel Chop Move (Direct Form)​

  • If Alice performs a Duplicitous Clue that doesn’t touch other good cards or provide immediate Tempo Fill-In information, it must have been done for some other reason.
  • After the Dupe reveals itself, the clued player can interpret the clue as a Trash CM on their own hand (as if it were Time Traveled Known Trash, from the future).
  • Thus, the clued player Chop Moves all cards that were to the right of the clued dupe.
  • For example, in a 3-player game:
    • All 2s are played.
    • Cathy holds a known Purple 3.
    • Alice clues 3 to Bob, newly touching a single card.
    • Bob plays his 3 and gasps when he sees it's Purple 3.
    • Since this was effectively a 0-for-1, Alice must have chosen this line in order to give Bob Known Trash.
    • Therefore, the team can all tell that Alice performed a Time Travel Chop Move. Bob Chop Moves all the cards that were behind his Purple 3.

The Time Travel Chop Move (Blind-Play Form)​

  • What if Alice wishes to perform a Time Travel Chop Move on Bob, but is unable to cleanly clue it directly by itself?
  • In such a case, Alice can still attempt to force the dupe to blind-play. She must choose carefully, though, to ensure that the end result is truly 'bad' enough to result in a full Time Travel Chop Move!
  • More specifically, such a blind-play must be sure to avoid the following conditions:
    • The clue can’t otherwise meet Minimum Clue Value Principle.
    • The clue can’t simply look like a normal Assisted Trash CM on the clue receiver.
  • For example, in a 4-player game:
    • All 1s are played. Donald has a known Blue 2.
    • Bob has a previously clued red card. Alice reclues it with a 3 clue, touching no other cards.
    • Bob blind-plays as a Self-Bluff, but is shocked when a Blue 2 is played.
    • Since this resulted in a 0-for-1 - and it wasn’t any sort of potential emergency - Bob understands that he should Chop Move everything that was behind his Blue 2, as a Time Travel Chop Move.
  • In advanced games, it's also possible to perform Time Travel Chop Moves further away from slot 1, by using certain 0-for-1 advanced techniques (such as Useless Clue Ejections, Bad TCCME,Trash Push UTD, etc).
